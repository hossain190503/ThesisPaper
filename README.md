# Thesis Paper
## ABSTRACT
This thesis investigates the design and optimization of lead-free perovskite solar cells utilizing CsGeIBr₂ as the absorber material, mitigating the environmental and toxicity issues linked to lead-based perovskites. The research is organized into three primary phases: density functional theory (DFT) calculations, device simulation utilizing SCAPS-1D, and performance prediction based on machine learning (ML). During the initial phase, DFT calculations were utilized to examine the electrical and optical characteristics of CsGeIBr2. The results showed that it had great optoelectronic properties, such as a suitable band-gap of 1.19 eV, a high absorption coefficient of 10⁵ cm⁻¹, and good charge carrier mobility of 50 cm²/Vs. Because of these qualities, CsGeIBr₂ is a good candidate for use in high-efficiency solar cells. In the second phase, SCAPS-1D simulations were used to make the solar cell structure better. We looked at different hole transport layers (HTLs), such as SnS, CTST, and CuI. CuI was the best HTL because it had better band alignment, lower recombination losses, and higher hole mobility. For the ETL (electron transport layer), WS₂ was chosen as the electron transport layer (ETL) because it is effective at extracting electrons and is compatible with the absorber. The optimized device achieved impressive performance: a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of 30.24%, an open-circuit voltage (VOC) of 0.906 V, a short-circuit current density (JSC) of 39.07 mA/cm², and a fill factor (FF) of 85.47%. These results show that CsGeIBr₂-based solar cells might be as good as or better than traditional lead-based perovskite solar cells. In the last step, machine learning (ML) was used to predict how well the solar cell would work with different manufacturing settings, like the thickness of the absorber and the concentration of the dopant. The ML model gave us useful information on how to make the fabrication process better, how to predict efficiency trends, and how to make things more scalable. This study shows that CsGeIBr2 could be a good choice for solar cells because it is a high-performance, lead-free perovskite material. By using DFT calculations, SCAPS-1D simulations, and machine learning together, it has come up with a systematic way to design and improve photovoltaic devices that are both efficient and good for the environment. The findings facilitate the advancement of economical, high-efficiency solar cells, aiding the global shift towards renewable energy. Future work will concentrate on experimental validation and the investigation of tandem solar cell architectures to further improve performance.

# METHODOLOGY

This methodology adopts a multi-scale approach to bridge the gap between fundamental material properties and device-level performance. The workflow is divided into three integrated stages: first-principles calculations to evaluate the absorber material, numerical device modeling to optimize the cell architecture, and data-driven forecasting to assess manufacturing scalability.
### Phase 1: First-Principles Calculations (DFT)
The foundational stage of this research relies on Density Functional Theory (DFT) to probe the electronic and optical "fingerprints" of $CsGeIBr_2$. While experimental data for lead-free perovskites can sometimes be inconsistent, DFT provides a relatively reliable baseline for intrinsic properties.I focused on calculating the electronic band structure and the density of states (DOS) to determine the band-gap. 
### Phase 2: Device-Level Simulation (SCAPS-1D)
Once the material constants were established, I moved to SCAPS-1D (Solar Cell Capacitance Simulator). This software is widely used because it effectively solves the Poisson equation and the continuity equations for electrons and holes.The goal here was to design a functional "stack." I didn’t just look at the absorber; I spent significant time evaluating the interfaces between the $CsGeIBr_2$ layer and various transport materials. For the Hole Transport Layer (HTL), I tested CuI, SnS, and CTST. The simulation suggests that CuI provides the most favorable band alignment, effectively "lining up" the energy levels to allow holes to pass through while blocking electrons. It is worth noting, however, that SCAPS-1D assumes ideal thin-film layers. In a real-world setting, grain boundaries and surface roughness would likely introduce recombination centers that these numerical models might overlook.
### Phase 3: Machine Learning (ML)
ForecastingThe final phase uses Machine Learning to move beyond static simulations. Traditional modeling shows you one result for one set of inputs, but ML allows us to see the "big picture" of how different variables interact.I generated a dataset from the SCAPS-1D results, using parameters like absorber thickness and doping concentration as features. By training a regression model (such as a Random Forest or Artificial Neural Network), I was able to forecast the power conversion efficiency (PCE) across thousands of virtual iterations. This approach is particularly useful for identifying the "sweet spot" in manufacturing—for instance, determining exactly when an increase in thickness stops improving light absorption and starts hindering charge collection due to increased internal resistance. This layer of the methodology attempts to simulate the unpredictability of the fabrication process.
